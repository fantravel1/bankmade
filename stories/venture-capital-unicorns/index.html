The Unicorn Revolution

How a single term changed venture capital forever. The story of Aileen Lee, the woman who invented "unicorn," and the female investors reshaping startup ecosystems, founder relationships, and ecosystem power dynamics.

Feature

One Word Changes Everything

In 2013, Aileen Lee published a TechCrunch article with a simple premise: there are very few venture-backed companies that reach $1 billion valuation, so we should call them "unicorns." The term was whimsical, deliberately so, and it captured something important about their rarity and magical properties. What Lee didn't anticipate was that "unicorn" would become the defining metric of venture success, reshaping how Silicon Valley measures achievement and how founders think about growth.

In 2013, there were fewer than 40 unicorns globally. By 2021, there were over 500. By 2025, there are thousands. The term spread like wildfire through the startup ecosystem, entering mainstream vocabulary. Startups began explicitly targeting "unicorn status." Investors began measuring their success by unicorns in portfolio. The venture capital game had been forever altered by a single girl's observation and clever naming convention.

Redefining Founder Relationships

Female venture investors have generally emphasized founder relationships over portfolio-wide optimization. Rather than viewing founder-investor relationships as transactional (give capital, get equity), they often approach it as mentorship and deep partnership. This perspective has proven prescient: founders cite "advisor quality" as determining factor in VC selection more than valuation in many cases.

Aileen Lee's Cowboy Ventures specifically targets "underestimated founders"—women, people of color, LGBTQ+ individuals historically excluded from venture networks. By building genuine relationships rather than extracting returns, she's created superior deal flow and outcomes. Her thesis: diversity in founding teams correlates with better performance and more durable companies.

Portfolio Construction Philosophy

Female VC investors often approach portfolio construction differently than male counterparts. Rather than concentrating bets in a few companies hoping for unicorns, they maintain broader diversification. This sounds conservative, but actually generates superior risk-adjusted returns. By backing more founders and companies, they capture broader exposure to emerging categories and identify overlooked opportunities earlier.

Kirsten Green's Forerunner Ventures, for example, took small positions in numerous consumer startups before most investors understood the potential of D2C disruption. This diversified approach to consumer investing provided exposure to Glossier, Warby Parker, Bonobos, and dozens of others. The concentrated bets of others may have created more unicorns, but Forerunner's diversification delivered superior overall returns.

Ecosystem Impact and Legacy

Beyond returns, female VC investors have reshaped venture ecosystems. They've backed female founders at higher rates, reducing historical gender bias in capital allocation. They've invested in underrepresented geographies, bringing Silicon Valley expertise to Asian and European ecosystems. They've pioneered new investing models—mega-funds, signaling funds, GPOs—that expand who can participate in venture.

Arlan Hamilton's Backstage Capital raised $19 million to invest in underestimated founders. While small compared to mega-VC funds, its mission-driven approach attracted capital from foundations, families, and impact investors. She proved that venture investing can advance social goals while generating returns—a thesis that has influenced how billions flow through venture ecosystem.

The "unicorn" has become shorthand for venture success, but female investors have expanded definition of what success means. They've proven that patient capital, relationship-driven investing, and mission alignment can outperform herd-chasing and unicorn-hunting. Their legacy will extend far beyond the companies they've backed—it's in the ecosystem they've built and the founders they've believed in before others did.
